# PRODUCT_BRIEF.md

**Product:** Resume Roaster

---

**USER:**
Active job seekers across white-collar industries (Big Tech, Banking & Investment Banking, Accounting & Consulting, Design & Creative, Research & Academia, Management & Operations) applying to multiple roles at once, from early-career candidates and career-changers to experienced professionals whose seniority isn't translating on paper.

**PROBLEM:**
Candidates submit resumes that fail silently — filtered out by ATS parsing before a human ever sees them, or skimmed past in seconds — with no feedback loop to explain why. 74% of surveyed job seekers have been surprised by a rejection or non-response despite feeling qualified, and when asked directly what's blocking their interviews, the single largest answer (34%) was simply "not sure."

**CURRENT SOLUTION:**
They ask friends, family, or colleagues (61% and 47% respectively) — feedback that's socially safe but low-signal, rated an average 2.1/5 for honesty, and dominated by generic advice ("quantify your impact") that respondents have already heard and can't act on. Paid coaches (12%) are accurate but slow and expensive; free AI tools (39%) are used but not trusted to be specific.

**AI OPPORTUNITY:**
This isn't a formatting-automation problem — it's a judgment problem. The intelligence has to (1) recognize when a bullet is written at the wrong seniority register for the target role, (2) translate institutional dialects (consulting jargon, academic CV language) into market-legible phrasing without the user having to know the target vocabulary, and (3) diagnose *why* a specific line would get filtered or skimmed past and produce a concrete rewrite — not just flag a keyword gap. A rules-based checker can count keyword overlap; only a model that understands both the resume's actual content and the target role's implicit expectations can tell someone their bullet "reads like a job description, not an accomplishment," and show them what it should say instead.

**SUCCESS LOOKS LIKE:**
A user pastes in a rejection-prone resume and a target job description, and instead of "add more metrics," they get: "this bullet reads as mid-level, here's the senior-level version" — and they actually rewrite it because the feedback named something true they couldn't see themselves. They come back for the next application because the tool told them something a friend never would.
